Vela

This is Vela. Vela is a weather app for extreme water sport enthusiasts. Using Adobe XD, Sketch, and Usability hub, I was able to take this app from conception to a clickable prototype.

Research

I wanted to understand what was already on the market. I felt that by understanding what was already being offered to consumers, it would better help me understand how to help those consumers reach their goals.

I did so by conducting a competitive analysis on two apps that are similar to Vela: Windy.app and Surfline

This research was essential in my design process. I performed content audits, google surveys, and a competitive analysis on two of the most popular sites. What you see above is the summary of this research. I came away from this with a few things in mind:

  1. Users love the real-time weather map this is provided by windy.app.
  2. A visual of the beach is essential for users to feel connected to the sea and the community.
  3. No one wants clutter!
  4. The demographic for this app does not want to spend more than 5 minutes on their phone.

Ideation

In order to better understand the information hierarchy and the navigation of this app, I created a site map.

Then, using the site map, I created low-fidelity wireframes.

User Research

With some wireframes finally drawn up, it was time to move into my first round of user testing. I felt that the layout was slightly too clunky. I couldn’t exactly put my finger on it, and that was where the card sort came in.

I organized the different features and aspects of Vela into one word categories and asked multiple participants to sort them into whatever groups made sense. The results confirmed my hypothesis and helped me to better understand where I went wrong and what changes I could make.

More Ideation

With the results of the card sort in front of me, I was able to revise the site map into it’s second iteration.

Then, using that site map, I created mid-fidelity wireframes.

More User Research

With my mid-fidelity wireframes done, I needed to do another round of user testing. I contacted 5 potential users (ages 24-35) for moderated in-person tests. I came away from this with multiple revisions in mind. Some of the most pressing revisions are:

  1. I needed to change the layout of the weather reports page. There was no need to have an extra screen before the information was displayed. The point of this app is to have users be quick!
  2. Participants had trouble finding the button to change the filters on the weather map. Using a different icon and some color will help users.
  3. Finding the new post button on the social page was challenging for participants.

Design

After the usability testing, it was time for high-fidelity design. I wanted to focus on the revisions I needed to make.

Color and Typography

Typography - Geneva - Geneva is a professional font that is able to bring a simple but fun personality.

Color - Deep blue and bright green - With the background being white and most of the text being black, some highlights of blue and green will encourage excitement and engagement, as well as match the app.

Summary

Working as the sole designer from conceptualization to development, there was a steep learning curve. Every step of the design process was a new challenge. I learned how to connect with users during an interview, the benefits of rapid prototyping, and the importance of user and peer suggestions. My biggest takeaway is that no matter what roadblock you run into, keep going. If you try hard enough, and approach each problem with a positive attitude, there is nothing you can't do.
